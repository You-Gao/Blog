# The Fifth Discipline

> "In the long run, the only sustainable source of competitive advantage is your organization's ability to learn faster than its competition."
> <figcaption class="blockquote-footer">The Fifth Discipline</figcaption>

The book, seen as a ground-breaking work by others, is seen as a bit too schizophrenic by me. Senge has a lot of ground to cover in too short of an amount of pages. As a reader, I can see the general goal: introduce the core concepts of "*systems-thinking*", then apply them to different business domains. 

But the pedagogical lucidity is simply lost in the flood of anecdotes and context-switching between business domains. What I wished more for in this book was conceptual clarity around what Senge means by "*systems-thinking*" before going on to apply its principles. 

## Systems Thinking 

Gestalt thinking, systems thinking, arrows/loopy diagrams, complexity science, or "*big-picture*" thinking; Whatever you call it, the core tenets backing this methodology are these supposed facts:

1. The world is incomprehensibly complex.
2. Time is linear, but processes are non-linear.
3. Formal or "*purely*" mathematical logic can not express it.
